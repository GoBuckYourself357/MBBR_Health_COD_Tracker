How it works
BOD (mg/L) = COD × (ratio ÷ 100)
BOD lbs/day = BOD × Flow (MGD) × 8.34

Direct BOD entries take priority over ratio estimate. Typical dairy BOD/COD: 0.20–0.35. This is lower than municipal wastewater (0.50–0.65) due to fats and refractory organics in dairy effluent.

Dairy MBBR — quick reference
Dairy wastewater characteristics
COD — typical range1,000 – 10,000 mg/L (highly variable — CIP cycles spike it)
BOD₅500 – 5,000 mg/L  ·  BOD/COD ratio typically 0.20–0.35 for dairy
Main organicsLactose, milk fat, whey proteins, casein — all highly biodegradable
Fat / oil / grease (FOG)Can coat biofilm media and block O₂ transfer — monitor closely
CIP (clean-in-place) eventsHigh pH, high COD, PAA or Cl₂ spikes — biggest shock load source in dairy
Ammonia sourceProtein breakdown (casein, whey) — Ammonia loading directly tied to protein content
Phosphorus sourceMilk naturally high in P — dairy wastewater typically 10–50 mg/L
Nitrogen — dairy-specific
Ammonia in MBBR< 1 mg/L excellent  1–5 mg/L acceptable  > 5 mg/L concern
Nitrite< 0.5 mg/L normal  0.5–1 mg/L elevated  > 1 mg/L nitrite lock
NitrateRises as NH₃ is oxidized — expected 10–30 mg/L in active nitrification
Dairy TKNTypically 50–200 mg/L raw — much higher than municipal due to protein load
Nitrification competitionHigh COD dairy wastewater — heterotrophs outcompete nitrifiers for O₂. Keep DO high.
Dissolved oxygen — dairy MBBR
DO for nitrification≥ 3–4 mg/L ideal  2–3 mg/L marginal  < 2 mg/L limiting
DO for high-COD dairyMay need 4–6 mg/L due to high O₂ demand from fat and protein oxidation
FOG impact on O₂Fats and oils reduce O₂ transfer efficiency — surface foam is a warning sign
DO saturation at 68°F9.1 mg/L (decreases with rising temp and increasing salinity)
O₂ demand from dairy COD~1.0–1.5 g O₂ per g COD removed
pH — dairy considerations
Nitrifier optimum7.5 – 8.0
Acceptable range6.8 – 8.5
Permit range6.5 – 9.5
CIP acid wash riskNitric acid or phosphoric acid CIP can crash pH below 6.5 — nitrification stops
CIP caustic wash riskNaOH CIP can spike pH above 9.5 — free ammonia toxicity and permit violation
Lactose fermentationOrganic acids from lactose breakdown naturally drop pH — monitor during high-load periods
Alkalinity — dairy MBBR
Healthy residual≥ 120 mg/L CaCO₃
Minimum for stability80 – 120 mg/L
Critical — supplement now< 80 mg/L
Consumed per mg/L NH₃ nitrified7.14 mg/L CaCO₃
Dairy alkalinity noteRaw dairy wastewater is often low in alkalinity — supplementation commonly required for stable nitrification
Common supplementsSodium bicarbonate (safest), lime, soda ash
Temperature
Optimal nitrification68 – 82°F
Dairy wastewater tempOften arrives warm (86–104°F from pasteurization / CIP) — may need cooling before MBBR
Above 95°FNitrifier activity begins to decline — AOB more sensitive than heterotrophs
Impaired (< 50°F)Significant rate reduction — common in winter dairy operations
Temperature correctionRate changes ~5.8% per °C (10.4% per °F) from 68°F baseline (θ = 1.058)
COD & organic loading — dairy
Typical dairy influent COD1,000 – 10,000 mg/L (vs 200–800 mg/L for municipal)
BOD/COD ratio for dairy0.20 – 0.35 — lower than municipal due to fats and refractory organics
Surface area loading rate (SALR)5 – 15 g COD/m²·day dairy design range (higher than municipal)
Nitrification SALR0.5 – 2.0 g Ammonia-N/m²·day
FOG loading concernFats > 100 mg/L can foul media and reduce effective surface area — pre-treatment DAF recommended
Whey / high-lactose eventsCan double or triple COD overnight — use shock load detection alerts
Solids & biofilm
TSS in dairy MBBRTypically 100–500 mg/L — higher than municipal due to organic solids
VSS/TSS volatile fraction70 – 85% healthy active biomass
Imhoff cone (60 min)< 2 mL/L good  2–5 mL/L moderate  > 5 mL/L high
Biofilm thickness target50 – 300 µm — thicker may cause O₂ diffusion limitation in high-COD dairy
Media fill30–70% of tank volume — do not exceed 70%
FOG on mediaFat coating reduces biofilm activity — visible as white/yellow slick on media surface
Phosphorus — dairy
Dairy P levelsTypically 10–50 mg/L in raw dairy WW — much higher than municipal (3–8 mg/L)
Minimum for biofilm growth≥ 0.1 mg/L — rarely limiting in dairy due to naturally high P
N:P ratio for healthy biomass~100:1 BOD : 1 P — dairy usually P-sufficient
Discharge concernHigh P in dairy effluent can cause eutrophication — check permit P limits
Volatile acids (VFA) — dairy
VFA:Alkalinity ratio< 0.1 stable  0.1–0.3 monitor  > 0.3 unstable
Dairy VFA sourceLactose ferments quickly to lactic and acetic acid — elevated VFA common after production surges
Lactic acidPrimary VFA from dairy — drops pH rapidly if alkalinity is insufficient
CIP acid contributionAcid CIP steps contribute directly to VFA — coordinate CIP discharge timing with plant ops
CIP chemical inhibition — dairy specific
Free chlorine (Cl₂)0.05–0.1 mg/L low risk  > 0.1 mg/L inhibitory to AOB
Peracetic acid (PAA)0.1–0.5 mg/L monitor  > 0.5 mg/L inhibitory
Dairy CIP frequencyTypically 1–3× per day — each event is a potential chemical shock to MBBR
Caustic NaOH (CIP)pH spike risk — dilute or equalize before entering MBBR
Iodophors (iodine sanitizers)Also inhibitory to nitrifiers — treat same as chlorine
Quaternary ammonium (QAC)Highly toxic to biofilm even at low concentrations — do not allow to enter MBBR
Best practiceEqualization tank before MBBR to blend and dilute CIP events — reduces shock loading significantly
Neutralizer stoichiometry
Sodium thiosulfate2.2 mg/L per mg/L Cl₂ or PAA
Sodium bisulfite1.5 mg/L per mg/L Cl₂ or PAA
Ascorbic acid2.5 mg/L per mg/L Cl₂ or PAA
Reaction timeThiosulfate and bisulfite react within seconds. PAA decomposition slower — allow adequate contact time before MBBR inlet.
Bacterial growth phases
Lag phaseBacteria adapting — common after CIP shock, startup, or temperature change
Exponential phaseRapid growth, high substrate utilization — ideal operating condition
Stationary phaseGrowth equals decay — stable biofilm, substrate may be limiting
Decline phaseNet negative growth — chemical toxicity, O₂ crash, or FOG fouling of media
Min SRT for nitrifiers3–10 days depending on temperature — critical in dairy where CIP events can washout nitrifier population
Dairy nitrifier recoveryAfter a toxic CIP event, nitrifier population can take 1–3 weeks to fully recover — expect elevated NH₃ during this period

Mix of bacteria, archaea, fungi, protozoa, and specialized organisms
Functional role Organisms (Genus/Order)

Organism stress indicators — what to watch
Nitrosomonas (AOB)Rising ammonia · DO below 2 mg/L · temp above 95°F or below 50°F · pH outside 7–8 · CIP shock
Nitrospira (NOB)Rising nitrite · DO below 1 mg/L — more O₂-sensitive than AOB
DenitrifiersRising nitrate · insufficient carbon (C:N below 3:1)
Heterotrophs competingVery high BOD loading — outcompete nitrifiers for O₂ and media space
Sphaerotilus (bulking)Filamentous — proliferates under low DO or low nutrients, causes sludge bulking
Sulfate-Reducing Bacteria (SRB)Rotten egg smell (hydrogen sulfide gas), metal corrosion — indicates anaerobic (oxygen-free) zones forming in the biofilm
Plant shutdown — intermittent aeration & dormancy
Used during emergency plant shutdowns or extended low-load periods (e.g. yeast contamination, weekend shutdowns with no wastewater operators)
What is intermittent aeration?Cycling blowers on and off (e.g. off 12 min / on 4 min) to keep bacteria alive on minimal oxygen while there is little or no food in the water. Prevents anaerobic crash while conserving energy.
DO target during cyclingAllow DO to drop to 0.8–1.0 mg/L during off cycle · spike to 2.0–2.5 mg/L during on cycle · never below 0.5 mg/L — Nitrospira dies faster than Nitrosomonas below this causing nitrite buildup on restart
Starvation foamingHeavy foam during shutdown = bacteria lysing when starved of food and oxygen. Cell contents release surfactants from dairy wastewater. Adding fresh wastewater provides substrate and resolves foam quickly — if it resolves in 1–2 hours the biofilm is healthy.
Anammox silver liningCandidatus Brocadia thrives in intermittent low-oxygen conditions — may become more active during shutdown periods
Watch forSphaerotilus filamentous bulking · SRB activity if anaerobic zones form · elevated nitrite when restarting (NOB recovery lag)
Restart procedure after shutdown
Step 1Restore full aeration first — bring DO back to 3+ mg/L before introducing wastewater
Step 2Ramp up flow gradually — start at 25–50% of normal flow for the first few hours
Step 3Monitor ammonia closely — expect a spike before nitrification recovers. Lag phase typically 1–5 days depending on shutdown duration.
Step 4Watch nitrite — if NO₂ rises above 1 mg/L, Nitrospira (NOB) is not keeping up. Hold at reduced load until nitrite drops.
Step 5Log a stress event in the Diag tab to document the shutdown period and restart date for future reference
Good signFoam and DO issues resolving within 1–2 hours of adding wastewater = healthy resilient biofilm — bugs went to sleep, did not die
